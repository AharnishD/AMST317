Gandhi’s Early Life & Background

Biography:

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi's (aka Mahatma Gandhi) life heavily revolved around the concept of non-violent resistance, coupled with his efforts to achieve independence for India. His philosophy, commonly known as satyagraha, or "truth-force," was rooted in the belief that non-violent resistance could bring about social and political change. Gandhi's early life of nonviolent resistance began in South Africa, where he fought against discriminatory laws targeting the Indian community. Upon returning to India in 1915, Gandhi became a prominent leader in the Indian National Congress and spearheaded several non-violent movements against British colonial rule. His legacy continues to inspire and act as the framework for non-violent movements worldwide; Even decades later, leaders such as Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela adopted Gandhi's principles in their own struggles for civil rights and social justice.

Gandhi's 6 Principles:

Nonviolence

Ahimsa — rejecting violence in all forms.

Truth

Satya — commitment to honesty and moral courage.

Self-Sacrifice

Brahmacharya — Willingness to suffer without retaliation.

Non-violent Resistance

Satyagraha — Resisting injustice through mass civil disobedience.

Self-Rule

Swaraj — Gandhi wanted self-governance for individuals and communities.

Well-being of all

Sarvodaya — A life devoted to serving humanity.
