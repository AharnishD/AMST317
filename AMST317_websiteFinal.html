Gandhi’s Early Life & Background

Biography:

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi's (aka Mahatma Gandhi) life heavily revolved around the concept of non-violent resistance, coupled with his efforts to achieve independence for India. His philosophy, commonly known as satyagraha, or "truth-force," was rooted in the belief that non-violent resistance could bring about social and political change. Gandhi's early life of nonviolent resistance began in South Africa, where he fought against discriminatory laws targeting the Indian community. Upon returning to India in 1915, Gandhi became a prominent leader in the Indian National Congress and spearheaded several non-violent movements against British colonial rule. His legacy continues to inspire and act as the framework for non-violent movements worldwide; Even decades later, leaders such as Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela adopted Gandhi's principles in their own struggles for civil rights and social justice.

Gandhi's 6 Principles:

Nonviolence

Ahimsa — rejecting violence in all forms.

Truth

Satya — commitment to honesty and moral courage.

Self-Sacrifice

Brahmacharya — Willingness to suffer without retaliation.

Non-violent Resistance

Satyagraha — Resisting injustice through mass civil disobedience.

Self-Rule

Swaraj — Gandhi wanted self-governance for individuals and communities.

Well-being of all

Sarvodaya — A life devoted to serving humanity.

Gandhi's Global Impact

Gandhi's impact in South Africa and India has inspired the likes of people all around the world.

Gandhi was crucial to what nonviolent social movement is today.

As has been demonstrated, Gandhi was the founding father of the nonviolent social movement, because he laid the very foundation for overturning unjust policies through mass civil disobedience. What started out as campaigns in South Africa against racial discrimination, later transformed into India’s independence, led to a domino effect of every neglected demographic of people to take a stand for themselves and fight corrupt governments. He developed his methodology through historical campaigns, religious thought, and writing on civil disobedience (Schock, 278). He looked into past rebellions–most notably the peasant rebellions in India and boycott campaigns in Bengal, struggles from other parts of the world, and combined them with his religious beliefs to bring about political reform (278). Gandhi realized that one person defying the rules isn't enough and that he needed a group of people to combat injustice; He is laying out the basis of a successful nonviolent social movement.
In America during the 1960’s, what we saw of the great successes from Dr. King’s nonviolent social movements were highly inspired by Gandhi’s methods of nonviolent movements; King gave direct commendation to Gandhi and believed when he visited India, that is when he started seeing a “boom” in Civil Rights activism. Cesar Chavez–who fought for Mexican-American labor rights, modeled his nonviolent activism with similar methods: boycotts, hunger strikes, and marches. Looking beyond America, we see Nelson Mandela–a South African anti-apartheid politician, who credited Gandhi for influencing the anti-apartheid movement in South Africa; He acknowledged Gandhi’s principles as key to victory against oppressive laws. We also see Gandhi’s impact in recent days with women’s rights movements who, like Tarana Burke, nonviolently protested for women’s rights to be enforced in the work space through social media. Other honorable mentions include: the student anti-Vietnam war movements in the USA and Australia,worker insurrection in France (1968), and a major push for democracy starting in the 1980s and spilling over into the 21st-century that motivated people to challenge unjust governments globally (Philippines, Chile, Europe, etc).
Gandhi laid the groundwork for other activists to create their own ideologies. Many scholars paid attention to Gandhian nonviolence resistances; one of them being Richard Gregg, who published a book (1971) inspired by his visit to India. He wrote out the psychological theory in Gandhi’s campaign, and developed the term “moral jiu-jitsu” to explain how Gandhi’s nonviolent campaigns led him to gain the moral high ground over the violent oppressors. “War Without Violence” written by Krishnalal Shridharani, an activist who supported Gandhi and participated in his movement, made a positive influence on the Congress of Racial Equality activists who were battling for civil rights in the South. People like Gene Sharp took what he learned from Gandhi and developed a military-esque methodology to add tactics to the previous philosophy.
Gandhi was highly influential despite his passing, and his perspectives can be used in today's crisis in Sudan. Gandhi believed that every individual, no matter their race or social hierarchy, had the right to peace, and the violence in Sudan disrespects these human values. Looking at this crisis with Gandhi’s lens should make us respond to the conflict nonviolently; We need to recognize that every civilian deserves to have peace and the conflict shouldn’t result in the taking of these basic rights. Humanity should come together as a whole to peacefully protest against the injustice in Sudan in order for the bloodshed to stop, or else countless lives will continue ending and Sudan’s hope for democracy will shatter. This is further explained by Schock who points out that with individual nonviolence there’s a social effect that promotes a better society, and if enough of these individuals display the same nonviolent behavior, then social change on a wider scale will occur (278).
All in all Gandhi’s framework serves as a paradigm to activists who see problems in their governments and teaches you how to combat them, the right way (peacefully). He laid the foundation to a successful nonviolent movement and inspired others to add their twist to it whether that being military-esque tactics, social media, or moral jiu-jitsu. Gandhi was crucial to what nonviolent social movement is today and he is a major reason as to why we see countries change for the better; brutal regimes overthrown into democratic governments. and although he passed over 70 years ago, his past work serves as a paradigm to what we need to do today; namely Sudan.
